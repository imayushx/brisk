THE CHECKLIST
SMART BUYERS USE.

Technical signals tell you when. Fundamentals tell you what. Here's how to use both together — and when to buy, hold, or sell.

WHEN TO BUY
✓
EPS is growing
Earnings per share going up = company making more money. That's the core of everything.
✓
ROE above 15%
Return on Equity tells you how efficiently a company turns investor money into profit. Above 15% is healthy.
✓
Debt/Equity below 0.6
Low debt means the company isn't stretched thin. If things go bad, they can survive. High debt companies are fragile.
✓
P/E is fair vs peers
You're not paying a crazy premium. P/E below 25 is generally reasonable — but compare within the same industry.
✓
Strong competitive moat
The company has something that's hard to copy — a brand, patents, network effects, switching costs. That's what protects future profits.
WHEN TO HOLD
~
Earnings are meeting targets
Not growing fast but not missing either. The story is intact. No reason to exit a good company mid-journey.
~
Pays consistent dividends
A company that keeps paying and growing its dividend is telling you something — it's confident in its own future.
~
Market leader in a growing sector
Even if the stock isn't exciting today, if it owns its industry and that industry is growing — patience gets rewarded.
~
The original reason you bought still holds
Ask yourself: has anything actually changed about this business? If no — sit tight. Price moves aren't the same as business changes.
WHEN TO SELL
✗
Earnings consistently missing
One bad quarter happens. Two or three in a row means something is broken. That's your signal to reassess.
✗
Debt rising rapidly
Companies that keep borrowing to fund operations — not acquisitions — are often papering over a problem. Watch debt levels quarterly.
✗
Price far beyond intrinsic value
When a stock's P/E is 80-100x and earnings aren't growing to match — you're holding air. Book the profit.
✗
Competitive moat is cracking
A new competitor is winning customers, the product is getting commoditised, or the industry is shifting against them. These are the slow-moving disasters to watch for.
✗
Down 7–10% from your buy price
The classic stop-loss rule. Cuts losses before they compound. Hard to do emotionally — but mathematically sound.
